2.5 Some Notes on the Remainder

(a) When the y-intercept in the sequence function is not zero or through the origin, strict inequality holds between whole function and its parts of summands. Whenever a part is made from the whole, the magnitude of the remainder increase by one in multiples, thus the partition frequency which is one less than the partition length is the multiple that composes the whole remainder after partitioning.

(b) It has been observed that the remainder is independent of all other terms of the sequence function except c, but this is true only and only if the gradient or gap, m is uniform, or else this magnitude of the y-intercept keeps changing to reflect the change in the gap as we shall dwell on for non linear functions of integer sequences or similarly, non-arithmetic sequences.

(c) There is a ratio

|c||m|⁢of the magnitude of the y-intercept to the magnitude of the gradient (8)

Since in the sequence function, Y=m⁢X+c, m represents the common difference between consecutive terms of the sequence, the ratio signifies the ordinals traversed in a single instant of partitioning, that is, per part made, from the whole integer, the remainder generated in turn generates c/m ordinals in the ranks. This is important in application of concepts where ordinals determine the worth of a transaction for example in distribution where returns may be enumerated in terms of ordinals, not nominals.

(d) In the context of arithmetic sequences, c or the y-intercept ought to be the first term of the sequence but sometimes, this term does not satisfy the properties of such numbers in a sequence. This confusion is compounded by being in position, 0 of the sequence whether zero-based or one-based indexing is used since it is anchored at X=0. Its value of X being 0 means that it does not contribute to the partition’s sum side of the equation but has a value as such on the whole side of the same equation and as such, we are met with the same problem as division by zero but this time, addition; both in Peano Arithmetic. From all this, we can vividly trace how the remainder of a partitioned sequence function comes about.

3 CO-SEQUENCES

Integer sequences are numbers in sequel that follow a predefined rule, formula and/or property. Some sequences are infinite, others finite but here we consider infinitude. Some properties of a sequence include first term, common difference or ratio or gap and what we have come to understand as the remainder term, c, the y-intercept if they can be represented on Cartesian geometry. So, for a co-sequence I propose to modify the common difference/ratio/gap generally. An important property of a co-sequence is that it maintains the remainder of its sequence as pertains partitioning. So, given a sequence function, Y=m⁢X+c, Y being the X⁢t⁢h term of the sequence. To construct a co-sequence of order, d, the new X⁢t⁢h term, Yd, we add Y to the product of X and d;

Yd=Y+X⁢d

Thus,

Yd =m⁢X+c+X⁢d (9)
Yd =m⁢X+X⁢d+c
Yd =X⁢(m+d)+c

Only the X⁢t⁢h term has changed but not X itself, thus we say that Yd is the X⁢t⁢h term in order, d. This construction is easier for arithmetic sequences with just functions since the difference, m is common compared to non-arithmetic sequences where it changes often. Real values of integer sequences can be used in both to obtain co-sequences. And as a result,

3.1 Proposition 3

The remainder of partitioning a sequence function in order d,(in a co-sequence) is the same as that of partitioning the same function in the zeroth, d=0, order.

Proof
Rewrite (2) in order d,

(m+d)⁢X+c =(m+d)⁢x1+(m+d)⁢x2+…+(m+d)⁢xk+c
(m+d)⁢X+c =(m+d)⁢(x1+x2+…+xk)+k⁢c
But x1+x2+…+xk=X
(m+d)⁢X+c =(m+d)⁢X+k⁢c
c =k⁢c
c−c =k⁢c−c
0 =k⁢c−c
0 =c⁢(k−1)

Since the result is the same as that in (2), proved.

3.2 Further Notes on Co-Sequences

(a) Since, co-sequences are constructed in order, d, which modifies the common(arithmetic) or uncommon(non-arithmetic) gap, we are led to say that sequences are of order zero, d=0, that is, m+d=m+0=m.

(b) Constructing a co-sequence can be both by functions and real values for arithmetic sequences and only real values for non-arithmetic sequences but it all follows from (9). Besides modifying the gap, other properties of the sequence may be lost in a co-sequence compared to those of its sequence, in a quest for a uniform/similar remainder. For example,

(i) The 10⁢t⁢h prime number is 29 which indeed is prime, but in the 10⁢t⁢h order, that is, d=10, the 10⁢t⁢h prime will be,

Y10 =Y0+X⁢d,X=10,d=10
Y10 =29+(10×10)
Y10 =29+100
Y10 =129⁢(which is composite)

(ii) The 10⁢t⁢h odd number is 19 but in the 10⁢t⁢h order, that is, d=10,

Y10 =Y0+X⁢d,X=10,d=10
Y10 =19+(10×10)
Y10 =19+100
Y10 =119⁢(which is still odd)

(c) Since the remainder does not change as pertains partitioning, both the ratio (8) and the ordinal or X-value of the remainder(c⁢(k−1)) will change due to change of course in gap. The latter decreases as the order increases as well as the former, for when the remainder direction is negative and vice versa otherwise. The essence of co-sequences thus lies in scaling of relations. The X-value or ordinal of the remainder obtained in a co-sequence can be recovered as it were in the original sequence by applying the formula in (9) to the remainder, so that it re-aligns with the original rank.

4 APPLICATION OF THE LINEAR FUNCTION MODEL TO NON-ARITHMETIC SEQUENCES

Some sequences have functions that cannot be described by a straight line, Y=m⁢X+c and as such, the remainder after partitioning is not directly evident except through trial and error with real values or other brute force methods. Here, we use the linear function model previously developed to address the following

(i) An approximation of the remainder term or the y-intercept, c,

(ii) From (i), the direction of the strict inequality can be derived

That non-arithmetic sequences inhere strict inequality can be easily deduced from J.L Jensen’s’ study of convex functions and the inequalities between their mean values, thus in a way, we extend the investigation into integer sequences. We shall endeavor to construct a line of best fit or perform a linear regression on real values of the sequence(s) so that we obtain an equation that contains the remainder term, c.

4.1 Linear Regression on a Sequence Function

With real values of independent and dependent variables and for our case in integer sequences, an ordinal, X, and nominal, X⁢t⁢h term or Y, we can ”fit” the data relationships on a straight line and obtain an arithmetic sequence function like equation. From the composition of the data, the values to be computed therefrom are the gradient or common difference, m and the y-intercept or remainder term, c. With these obtained, the straight line equation can be formed. But these computed values are statistical, thus approximate to and change often for different input ranges; so range is a critical factor here. We shall manually perform a linear regression using concepts of integer sequences such as gap and terms, for example, the gradient m may be the average gap between terms of the integer sequence in a given range, while the y-intercept, c which can be esoterically defined as the 0⁢t⁢h term is derived as in Cartesian geometry, from the product of gradient and the average of X-values subtracted from the average of the Y-values. As in,

m=sum of gaps/number of gaps (10)
c=average of Y values−m× average of X values (11)

Placing them together results in Y=m⁢X+c Thus what follows are examples that try to extract lines if best fit from non-arithmetic integer sequences in the range of the first 100 terms and therefore,

G.H Hardy and H.E Littlewood had conjectured about the prime counting function and proposed an inequality between the density of primes from 2 to a range x and from any other starting point, counting or searching within the same width x, the clearest that can be extracted being

ρ*⁢(x)=limn→∞(π⁢(n+x)−π⁢(n)) (16)

*ρ(x)=ρ([x]) is the greatest number of primes that occurs indefinitely often in a sequence, n+1,n+2,…,n+[x] of [x] consecutive integers.
